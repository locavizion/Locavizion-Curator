03 · How to Submit Your Knowledge

Simple questions.
Powerful answers.

We don't ask you to write essays or fill in complicated forms. We ask you simple questions — the same ones you'd answer naturally if a friend were visiting. The structure behind those answers is our job, not yours.

For each place, experience, or insight you want to share, answer these five prompts. That's it. We do the rest.

1
What is it — and what makes it genuinely worth knowing?
Not the official description. Your description. What would you tell a friend in two sentences? What makes this place different from the version a tourist would find on their own?
"Rooster and the Till is a 40-seat farm-to-table in Seminole Heights. The pork belly changes weekly and it's been the best restaurant in Tampa Bay two years running — most visitors have never heard of it."
2
When should someone go — and when should they avoid it?
This is the insight that no app has. The timing tip that turns a mediocre visit into a memorable one. Be specific. Day of week, time of day, season, what changes when, and what most people get wrong.
"Tuesday or Wednesday evening. Weekends have a 90-minute wait and feel rushed. On a weekday you walk straight in and the kitchen has time for you."
3
Who is it best for — and what situation does it suit?
Solo traveler? Business dinner? First date? Post-conference wind-down? Context is what separates a recommendation from a search result. The more specific you are about who and when, the more useful your insight becomes.
"Great for a solo dinner or a small group that wants to talk. Not ideal for a loud celebration — it's intimate and the service is attentive, not rushed."
4
What should they know that isn't written anywhere?
The off-menu item. The table to ask for. The thing the staff will tell you if you ask. The cultural context that makes the experience land differently. This is your real contribution — the local intelligence that no search engine can surface.
"Ask for the pasta special — it's not on the menu and it changes weekly. The bar seats have the best view of the open kitchen if you're solo."
5
Where does it fit in a local flow — what comes before or after?
Locals don't experience the city in isolated stops. They move through it. Help us understand how this place connects to the neighbourhood, the evening, the day. What makes it a starting point, a midpoint, or an ending?
"Start here for dinner, walk 8 minutes to Gin Joint for cocktails, then catch the streetcar to Ybor if the night is going well. Perfect sequence for a Wednesday."

05 · Best Practices

How to make
the platform work for you.

The Curators who earn most are not the ones with the biggest following. They are the ones who show up specifically, share genuinely, and let the platform's reach do what their personal audience alone cannot. A few things that make a real difference.

1
Be specific. Always more specific than you think you need to be.
Generic knowledge is what every other platform already has. "Great atmosphere" is worthless to us. "Go on a Tuesday at 7pm, sit at the bar, order the short rib — the server will tell you it's sold out but it isn't" is the kind of thing travelers book sessions to find out. The more specific your submission, the more useful it becomes, and the more your profile stands out.
2
Let your audience know you are a Locavizion Curator — without pitching them.
Post your perspective on your city the way you already do. Mention casually that you do Curator briefings for people visiting — not as a promotion, as a fact. "If you're coming to Tampa and want to know what's actually worth seeing, I do short briefings now through Locavizion" does more than any sales post. The people who already follow you already trust you. That trust converts.
3
Design one experience and market it before you need to.
Don't wait for bookings to design the experience — design it first and post about it. Tell people what they'll see, feel, and leave knowing. The title matters: "Tampa Street Art & Culture Walk" is fine. "The Ybor City you weren't supposed to find" is something people actually book. Make the experience specific to a feeling or a discovery, not just a category.
4
Submit 10 insights to start — then update quarterly.
Ten solid, specific, tagged insights across your domain gives the platform enough to surface you meaningfully in responses. Quality over quantity, always. Then come back every three months to update what's changed — what closed, what opened, what shifted. A Curator whose knowledge is current earns more because their recommendations are reliable.
5
After a briefing, ask one question.
At the end of every session, ask: "Is there anyone you know coming to Tampa soon who might find this useful?" Not a hard sell — a genuine question. Word of mouth from a satisfied traveler is more powerful than any marketing. One good session, followed by one honest question, generates two more sessions more reliably than any promotion campaign.
